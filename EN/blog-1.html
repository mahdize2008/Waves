Practical Guide to Reducing Human Cyber Risks 2022 - Human Risk Management - Part I

Employees have long been considered the "weakest link" in the cyber security chain of a business or organization, and considering that "human error is still the number one cause of data breaches", we decided to create the first practical guide available through futurewaves.tech .We share it in this context to help improve the security level of organizations and businesses.
Even as organizations devote more time and money to addressing human cyber risks, employee-related security incidents will continue to plague businesses in 2022. But why was that?
Simply put, many businesses are not doing enough to combat evolving threats. As cybercriminals use more advanced techniques to exploit humans, the traditional path of once-a-year security awareness training to protect today's businesses from the loss of sensitive information, credit damage, and consequences Not enough money. It is not bad to pay attention to some statistical examples to better understand the importance of the issue:

  • Estimates show that in 2021, a cyber attack will occur every 11 seconds.
  • Cyber attacks were predicted to reach $6 trillion in annual losses by 2021, doubling since 2015.
  • The average cost to businesses due to cyber attacks is $3.86 million and it takes more than 200 days to identify breaches.

The good news is that the solution to improving user security has evolved in recent years through the introduction of Human Risk Management (HRM), offering a much stronger level of protection for businesses of all sizes and sectors.
In this practical guide, we discuss why employees are one of the main insider threat factors and how to direct employee culture towards safe behavior in your business.

Humans - the #1 cause of cyber security breaches

  • About 90% of data breaches are related to human errors.
  • Negligent employees cause about 62% of security incidents.
  • 60% of organizations have more than 20 insider attacks in a year.
  • 98% of organizations are vulnerable to internal threats.

What are the main types of insider threats?

  • Careless insiders

    For example, an employee sending the wrong email or accidentally attaching the wrong file is the most common threat to your business, accounting for 62% of all security incidents.

  • Careless insiders with stolen authentication information

    For example, an employee whose username and password are exposed on the dark web accounts for 25% of all security incidents.

  • malicious insiders

    For example, employees or former employees with motivations detrimental to the business, such as a disgruntled employee who was recently fired, accounted for 13 percent of incidents.

Why are employees an insider threat?

Humans make mistakes

We all make mistakes. In fact, 43 percent of employees say they've made a mistake at work that compromised cybersecurity, such as sending the wrong email. The problem is that these types of "small" mistakes can lead to the disclosure of sensitive data and an opportunity for an attacker.

Humans are the target

Much of your business information, including suppliers, contractors, and customers, can be found online. This makes it easy for attackers to impersonate internal and external contacts, and all it takes is one successful trick to put your business at serious risk.

Humans break the rules

People in any business can break the rules, either maliciously or accidentally. But the vast majority of offenders go beyond violating password policies. Some employees can go so far as to steal company or organization data and sell it on the dark web.

Four key reasons for user data breaches

  • 1. human mistake

    An employee mistake, like a simple typo, can seem small, but the consequences can be huge. For many businesses, a breach related to human error results in fines, loss of customer trust, and loss of access to data.

    • Common ways that high-risk employee behavior can lead to a security incident
    • Sharing, writing or reusing passwords across multiple accounts
    • Mishandling of data, such as entering the wrong email recipient or attaching the wrong file
    • Lack of awareness of common threats, such as phishing emails
    • Failing to understand that security is the responsibility of all employees, not just a problem for the IT department

  • 2. Weakness of users against phishing attacks

    One of the most common ways an employee causes a security breach is by being exposed to a phishing attack. And with phishing becoming more targeted and sophisticated than ever, it's becoming harder for employees to detect these attacks.
    Some clever techniques attackers use to control your employees:

    • Spear phishing is a specific and targeted attack on one or a select number of victims, while regular phishing attempts to defraud the masses. In spear phishing, fraudsters often use social engineering and fake emails to target specific people in an organization.
    • Business Email Compromise: BEC attacks, also known as Business Email Compromise or BEC for short, are among the growing fraud methods by which hackers spoof the email addresses of high-level executives and send emails to Employees post and ask them to make financial transactions or disclose sensitive and important information.
    • Domain spoofing: Domain spoofing is a form of phishing in which an attacker spoofs the website or email domain of a well-known business or person in order to trick people into gaining their trust.

  • 3. Misuse of employee authentication information

    Employee passwords play a large role in security incidents, with 61% of breaches involving stolen credentials.
    Procedures for misusing employee authentication information:

    • The employee uses the same organizational username and password to log in to several third-party services.
    • A third-party service encounters a data breach and exposes user information.
    • User information is sold on the dark web, which attackers can potentially use to gain access to multiple user accounts.
      More than 22 billion records were added to the dark web in 2020.
      60% of the information on the dark web can potentially harm companies and organizations.

  • 4. Lack of security policies and processes

    An organization's information security policies help guide employee behavior in keeping IT systems secure. Without these policies, employees are less likely to know who to report phishing attacks to or who has access to sensitive data.

How can policies help reduce risk?

  • They protect your organization's critical information by clearly delineating employee security responsibilities.
  • They prevent the unauthorized disclosure, disruption, loss, access, use or alteration of an organization's information assets.

Human Risk Management

**Transform your organization's security culture through continuous human risk management (HRM).**
As we mentioned, user data breaches are still very common. That's because, traditionally, businesses have focused primarily on securing endpoints, networks and devices, etc., while neglecting their most valuable asset—people.
Even with increased attention and investment in security awareness initiatives, such as annual training and phishing simulation campaigns, this alone has not been enough to combat evolving threats. Today's organizations need a modern solution to strengthen their human resources.
HRM is a modern solution for empowering employees to combat evolving cyber threats, prevent user-related breaches and help businesses meet today's stringent standards. Human risk management is a new class of user-centric security that enables businesses to understand, mitigate and monitor the cyber risk of their employees. Humans are your strongest line of defense, but cybersecurity awareness training alone won't change user behavior. Human risk management (HRM) is the solution to creating a security-aware workforce.
The goal of a "security culture" is to encourage all employees to think and approach security through the same values, and encourage people to follow desirable behaviors that keep employees, customers, and suppliers safe. Building this culture requires several factors including stability, time and effort. Here are some key building blocks for creating a security-aware workforce.
Get support from above:
Executive support is critical to the success of any human risk management initiative. We don't just mean the CEO sending out emails to everyone—we mean the leadership team showing their full support for the initiative through regular communication with employees, allocating the necessary funds, and creating specific tasks.
Treat security as everyone's responsibility:
Employees need to understand that cybersecurity is the responsibility of every employee, not just the IT department. Employees have more access to computers and online resources than ever before and are widely seen as an organization's biggest risk. It is important that people understand the importance of working together to achieve cyber security.
Consistency and commitment are key:
The key to creating and maintaining a security-aware workforce is in the form of continuous and long-term user training, where continuous security awareness training has been proven to reduce the likelihood of employees engaging in threats such as phishing from 60% to 10% in the first year. Gives.

  • Go beyond security awareness training:

    Security awareness training is a great way to reduce human cyber risks, with 80% of organizations seeing a reduction in phishing vulnerability when employees are trained. But to build a resilient workforce against evolving threats, policies must be implemented and practical assessments, such as phishing simulations, must be conducted regularly.

  • Time, not budget, is the barrier to defeat:

    According to the SANS 2021 Human Cyber Risk Management report, 75 percent of security professionals spend less than half of their time on security awareness, knowing that more education is associated with increased positive behavior change.

  • Strategic alignment is very important:

    SANS also reports that managers need to make long-term, strategic investments in human resources, just as they do for other security efforts such as vulnerability management, incident response or security operations centers.
